AgentSLR: Automating Systematic Literature Reviews in Epidemiology with Agentic AI

Paper Dataset AgentSLR Toolkit
Shreyansh Padarha1, Ryan Othniel Kearns1, Tristan Naidoo2, Lingyi Yang3, Łukasz Borchmann4, Piotr Błaszczyk5, Christian Morgenstern2,
Ruth McCabe2, Sangeeta Bhatia2, Philip H. Torr1, Jakob Foerster1, Scott A. Hale1, Thomas Rawson2, Anne Cori2, Elizaveta Semenova2, Adam Mahdi1
1 University of Oxford  ·  2 Imperial College London  ·  3 University of Nottingham  ·  4 Snowflake AI Research  ·  5 Independent

Systematic literature reviews are essential for evidence-based public health, but they are costly, slow, and difficult to scale. In epidemiology, that creates a real bottleneck for outbreak preparedness and rapid evidence synthesis. AgentSLR automates the full systematic review workflow for epidemiology, from article retrieval and screening to structured extraction and report drafting.

Key Findings: Across nine WHO-designated priority pathogens, AgentSLR delivers expert-comparable performance while being 58× faster, cutting review time from roughly 7 weeks to about 20 hours. It provides end-to-end coverage across retrieval, screening, extraction, and report synthesis, while human-in-the-loop oversight remains important for catching stage-specific failure modes.

Processing Time: AgentSLR vs. Human Review

Wall-clock time (hours) for AgentSLR vs. human annotators across pipeline stages, broken down by abstract screening, PDF conversion, full-text screening, and data extraction.

  • AgentSLR completes a full review in 20 hours versus 385 hours of manual effort, a 19.3× speedup in active time
  • Running continuously, this translates to less than 1 calendar day versus 48.1 human workdays, a 58× reduction

Model Performance Across Pipeline Stages

Macro F1 for five frontier models across all pipeline stages. Coloured dots show Flagging, Counts, and Extraction sub-task scores within each extraction panel.

  • Kimi-K2.5 leads abstract screening (F1 = 0.77) and parameter extraction (F1 = 0.87); no single model dominates every stage
  • The performance gap across models narrows during extraction, suggesting structured tool-calling partially offsets raw model capability differences
  • GPT-OSS-120B, the smallest model tested, stays within 4.5 F1 points of the best model across all stages

Cost vs. Performance Trade-off

Average macro F1 across all pipeline stages plotted against estimated total cost per pathogen run (USD, log scale). Vertical bars show one standard deviation in F1 across stages.

  • GPT-OSS-120B matches GPT-5.2 in overall F1 (0.70 vs. 0.69) at over 96× lower cost ($13.9 vs. $1,348)
  • Kimi-K2.5 achieves the best overall performance (F1 = 0.74) at a mid-range cost of $277, making it the strongest value option

Article Screening Strategies

We show recall performance across seven pathogens for three screening strategies: two-stage AI screening, direct full-text AI screening, and human-conditioned full-text screening. Bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals shown (10,000 resamples).

  • Skipping abstract screening and processing full-texts directly raises recall from 0.81 to 0.89, with no human involvement required
  • Adding human abstract triage pushes recall to 0.92, the ceiling across all strategies, at the cost of manual effort

Implications

  • Human-in-the-Loop is the Right Mode: High recall ensures relevant evidence reaches validators. False positives are easy to remove, giving a net reduction in effort
  • Open-Weight Models are More Sustainable: Version pinning and local deployment matter for long-running living reviews where reproducibility across model updates is a scientific requirement
  • Broad Content Restrictions Pose Real Risk: Claude Opus 4.5 and Sonnet 4.5 refused all pathogen-related queries. Filters applied too broadly can block entire model families from legitimate public-health research
  • Living Reviews Become Feasible: Reducing the per-update burden makes continuous evidence curation practical, with direct benefits for pandemic preparedness
  • Error Structure Matters as Much as Frequency: Systematic gaps in coverage introduce bias. Characterising failure modes, not just rates, is essential before high-stakes deployment
