About AgentSLR

AgentSLR is a collaboration between the Reasoning with Machines Lab (OxRML) at the University of Oxford and the Pathogen Epidemiology Review Group (PERG) at the MRC Centre for Global Infectious Disease Analysis, Imperial College London.

The project brings together epidemiologists, computer scientists, and AI researchers to tackle one of the most resource-intensive tasks in evidence-based public health: systematic literature reviews.

Systematic reviews are essential for synthesising scientific evidence, yet they are costly, difficult to scale, and extremely time-intensive. AgentSLR automates the complete workflow — from article retrieval and multi-stage screening, through structured data extraction, to evidence synthesis via written reports.

Applied to epidemiological reviews of nine WHO-designated priority pathogens and validated against expert-curated ground truth, AgentSLR achieves screening decisions and data extractions consistent with human annotators while completing this process 58× faster (reducing review time from ~7 weeks to 20 hours).

The pipeline is open-source and open-weight, enabling reproducible and scalable evidence synthesis for outbreak preparedness and response.

Article Search & Retrieval

Data Sources: OpenAlex (30 req/s), PubMed (standard API), Europe PMC (20 req/s)

Search Strategy: Boolean queries with epidemiological terms, wildcard expansion for term variants, pathogen-specific exclusion criteria.

Process: Deduplication by DOI/PMID/PMCID, cascading PDF retrieval from open access sources, parallel downloads with rate limiting.

Example Query: [PATHOGEN] AND (transmissi* OR epidemiolog* OR model* OR severity OR "case fatality ratio*" OR "serial interval*" OR "reproduction number*" OR outbreak* ...)

Title & Abstract Screening

Inclusion Criteria:

  • English-language peer-reviewed research
  • Contains quantitative outbreak data
  • Reports transmission models or epidemiological parameters
  • Estimates key metrics (R0, serial interval, CFR, etc.)

Exclusion Criteria:

  • Conference proceedings, abstracts only
  • In-vitro studies without human/animal data
  • Case studies with <10 cases

Full-text Screening

Data Extraction

Extraction Categories:

  • Models: Model structure, assumptions, interventions simulated
  • Outbreaks: Location, timeline, case counts, demographics

Report Generation

Generation Process: Descriptive statistics and visualizations, evidence tables with traceability to source articles, LLM narrative synthesis.

Self-Refinement (K=5 iterations): Rubric-based critique across 8 dimensions (completeness, clarity, evidence grounding, etc.), targeted revision, verification of claims.

Output Reports:

  • Models review (architecture, interventions)
  • Outbreaks review (timeline, geography)
  • Parameters synthesis (quantitative estimates with uncertainty)

Key Findings

  • 58× Calendar-Time Savings: Under 1 day versus 48.1 human workdays, with full-text screening 118× faster than a human reviewer.
  • Cost vs. Performance: GPT-OSS-120B achieves competitive performance (F1 = 0.70) at $13.9 per run. GPT-5.2 costs 96× more and scores lower.
  • Flexible Screening: Two-stage AI screening achieves 0.81 recall. Skipping abstract screening and going direct to full-text recovers this to 0.89; human abstract triage improves it further to 0.92.
  • Parameter Extraction Ceiling: No model exceeds F1 = 0.63. The bottleneck is task ambiguity and reporting heterogeneity across papers, not model capability.
  • Experts Agree it Works: Six epidemiologists rated field-level accuracy at 0.80 on average, 18.8 percentage points above automated scores, and confirmed a net reduction in annotation effort.

Implications

  • Human-in-the-Loop is the Right Mode: High recall ensures relevant evidence reaches validators. False positives are easy to remove, giving a net reduction in effort.
  • Open-Weight Models are More Sustainable: Version pinning and local deployment matter for long-running living reviews where reproducibility across model updates is a scientific requirement.
  • Broad Content Restrictions Pose Real Risk: Claude Opus 4.5 and Sonnet 4.5 refused all pathogen-related queries. Filters applied too broadly can block entire model families from legitimate public-health research.
  • Living Reviews Become Feasible: Reducing the per-update burden makes continuous evidence curation practical, with direct benefits for pandemic preparedness.
  • Error Structure Matters as Much as Frequency: Systematic gaps in coverage introduce bias. Characterising failure modes, not just rates, is essential before high-stakes deployment.

Future Directions

  • Human Uplift Study: A formal study to quantify time savings in a human-in-the-loop workflow, prioritising understudied diseases with serious epidemic potential.
  • Multi-Domain Extension: The framework is domain-agnostic. Future work can explore generalisation across medical, social, and physical sciences.
  • Heterogeneous Multi-Agent Pipelines: Different models excel at different stages. Routing sub-tasks to models with complementary strengths could improve overall performance.
  • Systematic Error Analysis: Consistent underperformance on certain pathogens and study types warrants explicit characterisation before deployment in high-stakes settings.
  • Cross-Lingual Support: Current English-only screening excludes non-English evidence. Multilingual models offer a clear path forward, along with human annotated ground-truth.
